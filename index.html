No surface required

You already know how to use it.

AirType maps the QWERTY finger movements that live in your muscle memory — it doesn't ask you to learn a new input language. Put the gloves on, lock to a home-row reference, and type the way you always have. Anywhere.

Type on anything

Phones, laptops, AR glasses, a TV across the room. If it pairs with a Bluetooth keyboard, it works with AirType — no app, no driver.

Ten fingers, full speed

Not tap-one-letter-at-a-time. Real touch-typing posture, so trained typists keep the throughput they already have.

Keyboard ↔ mouse

Bring two fingers together to enter mouse mode — point, left-click, right-click — then spread back to the home row to keep typing.

How it works

Reconstructing the keyboard from your hands.

Surfaceless typing throws away the two things a keyboard provides — a hard surface that marks the instant of a keystroke, and keycaps that fix finger position. The whole system exists to rebuild those signals from body sensing alone.

Sense

Per-finger flex sensors read joint bend, a 9-axis IMU captures hand tilt and the downward tap, and a back-of-hand module fuses the signals in real time.

Decode

A probabilistic classifier turns motion into a distribution over keys, and a language model resolves the most likely word from the noisy stream — sub-50ms, on-device.

Calibrate

A green-LED step arms the system only once both hands lock to a home-row reference and the model is confident — so it never fires on stray motion.

Emit

Clean QWERTY keystrokes go out as a standard Bluetooth HID keyboard. Every phone, computer, and headset receives them with zero setup.

Flex-sensor finger

A conductive bend strip is laminated between the outer knit and the lining — invisible, washable, and it reads joint angle directly.

Back-of-hand module

One small board fuses every sensor, runs the decoder on a low-power NPU, and emits standard Bluetooth HID keystrokes — no app, no driver.

Noisy motion → clean key

The classifier turns each sloppy tap into a probability over keys; the language model collapses it to the word you meant.

Why now

Four curves just crossed.

The idea of typing without a keyboard is old. What's new is that the platform shift, the validating incumbent, cheap sensing, and language-model decoding all arrived in the same 18 months — and the most attractive lane is still open.

01 · PLATFORM

The platform moved

XR device shipments grew 44.4% in 2025. These devices ship with no surface to type on — text entry is their biggest unsolved problem.

02 · VALIDATION

The category got validated

Meta now ships an sEMG neural wristband as input for its display glasses — proof that "type without a keyboard" is a real product line.

03 · SENSORS

The sensors got cheap

9-axis IMUs and flex sensors cost pennies, and on-device NPUs make sub-50ms inference possible. The glove can finally be light, fast, and private.

04 · DECODER

The decoder exists

Language-model decoding — the same advance behind today's AI — turns sloppy, surfaceless motion into clean QWERTY. The piece that stalled every 2010s attempt.

The actual moat

The decoder, not the glove.

Each finger covers two to four keys and air motion is sloppy, so this can't be deterministic key-by-key detection. A statistical decoder paired with a language model resolves intent from a noisy signal — and decoder quality is the defensible asset that compounds with data.

Roadmap

Concept → MVP in four phases.

A staged path from breadboard to a two-hand product, de-risking the decoder with real data at every step.

Breadboard & first corpus · 0–3 months

Single-finger rig, sensor selection, and the first labelled typing dataset to prove the signal.

Single-hand MVP · 3–9 months

One instrumented glove, home-row decoder, Bluetooth HID out — typing real words on a real device.

Two-hand + air mode · 9–18 months

Full QWERTY across both hands, surfaceless "air" typing, calibration + language-model decoding.

XR & accessibility GTM · 18+ months

Harden the product, partner on XR text entry, and ship the assistive-input beachhead.
